Introduction to Political Parties

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Evaluate the role played by political parties in Texas
  • Understand how parties solve collective action problems

At some point, most of us have found ourselves part of a group trying to solve a problem, like picking a restaurant or movie to attend or completing a big project at school or work. Members of the group probably had various opinions about what should be done. Some may have even refused to help make the decision or to follow it once it had been made. This kind of collective action problem is very common in societies, as groups and entire societies try to solve problems or distribute scarce resources.

üèõÔ∏è Political Party
Political parties are groups of people with similar interests who work together to create and implement policies. They do this by gaining control over the government by winning elections. Party platforms guide members of the Legislature in drafting legislation. Parties guide proposed laws through the Legislature and inform party members how they should vote on important issues.
üîç Click to explore: What functions do political parties serve?

+30 points! üìã Party functions revealed!

Political parties perform several crucial functions: They nominate candidates to run for state government, Congress, and the presidency. They coordinate political campaigns and mobilize voters. Party platforms guide legislation, and parties inform members how to vote on important issues. While interest groups work indirectly to influence leaders, political parties try to directly influence public policy through members who seek to win and hold public office.

In Federalist No. 10, written in the late eighteenth century, James Madison noted that the formation of self-interested groups, which he called factions, was inevitable in any society, as individuals started to work together to protect themselves from the government. Interest groups and political parties are two of the most easily identified forms of factions in the United States.

üîç Click to explore: What is a party platform?

+30 points! üìú Platform politics explained!

Parties accomplish their goals by identifying and aligning sets of issues that are important to voters in the hopes of gaining support during elections. Their positions on these critical issues are often presented in documents known as a party platform, which is adopted at each party's presidential nominating convention every four years. If successful, a party can create a large enough electoral coalition to gain control of the government. Once in power, the party is then able to deliver the policy preferences its voters and elites choose.

Party Structure and Organization

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Describe the evolution of political parties in Texas
  • Understand the three components of party structure

A key fact about the U.S. political party system is that it's all about the votes. If voters do not show up to vote for a party's candidates on Election Day, the party has no chance of gaining office and implementing its preferred policies. For much of their history, the two parties have been adapting to changes in the size, composition, and preferences of the U.S. electorate.

üë• Party-in-the-Electorate
The party-in-the-electorate are those members of the voting public who consider themselves to be part of a political party and/or who consistently prefer the candidates of one party over the other. These voters are often called party identifiers, since they usually represent themselves in public as being members of a party. Party identifiers make up the majority of the voting public ‚Äì historically, over half of American adults call themselves "Republican" or "Democrat."
üîç Click to explore: How do generations differ in party identification?

+30 points! üìä Generational trends revealed!

Generation affects party identification significantly. Millennials (aged 21‚Äì37) are more likely to identify as or lean towards the Democratic Party and less likely to favor Republicans than are their Baby Boomer parents and grandparents (born between 1946 and 1964). Even among self-proclaimed independents, the overwhelming majority claim to lean in the direction of one party or the other, suggesting they behave as if they identified with a party during elections even if they preferred not to publicly pick a side.

üèõÔ∏è Party Organization
The party organization is the formal structure of the political party, and its active members are responsible for coordinating party behavior and supporting party candidates. It is a vital component of any successful party because it bears most of the responsibility for building and maintaining the party "brand." It also plays a key role in helping select, and elect, candidates for public office.
üîç Click to explore: What do county-level party organizations do?

+30 points! üèòÔ∏è Local party work revealed!

The county-level organization is the workhorse of the party system, especially around election time. It takes on many basic responsibilities: identifying and mobilizing potential voters and donors, identifying and training potential candidates for public office, and recruiting new members for the party. County organizations are also responsible for finding volunteers to serve on Election Day, either as officials operating the polls or as monitors ensuring elections are conducted honestly and fairly.

‚öñÔ∏è Party-in-Government
The party-in-government consists of party identifiers who have been elected or appointed to hold public office. Republican meetings are called party conferences and Democrat meetings are called party caucuses. Members meet in closed sessions to discuss legislative agenda items and make decisions about committee assignments. They also elect party leaders and party whips.
üîç Click to explore: What challenges does the party-in-government face?

+30 points! üèõÔ∏è Governing challenges revealed!

The U.S. government's separation of powers and federalism create problems for maintaining party unity. Each level and unit of government has different constituencies that office holders must satisfy. A legislator may serve a constituency that disagrees with the party executive on key matters of policy. For issues important enough to voters, like gun control or abortion rights, an office holder may feel their job will be in jeopardy if they too closely follow the party line. The Civil Rights Act of 1964, supported by Presidents Kennedy and Johnson (Democrats), saw many Republicans vote in favor while many southern Democrats opposed it.

History of Political Parties in Texas

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Describe the history and trends of Texas' major political parties
  • Understand how Texas shifted from Democratic to Republican dominance

The 19th-century culture of Texas was heavily influenced by the plantation culture of the Old South, dependent on African-American slave labor, as well as the patron system once prevalent in northern Mexico and South Texas. In these societies, the government's primary role was seen as being the preservation of social order.

üîµ Historical Democratic Dominance
From 1848 until Richard M. Nixon's victory in 1972, Texas voted for the Democrat candidate for president in every election except 1928, when anti-Catholic sentiment against Al Smith drove voters to Republican Herbert Hoover. A full century of Democratic Governors stretched between the departure of Republican Governor E.J. Davis (1874) and the election of Republican William P. Clements (1979).
üîç Click to explore: Who was Norris Wright Cuney?

+35 points! üë§ A Texas political pioneer!

Norris Wright Cuney (1846‚Äì1898) was one of the most influential black leaders in the South during the 19th century. In the post-Civil War era, he rose to leadership of the Republican Party in Texas. His mentor, George T. Ruby, was a black community organizer and director in the federal Freedmen's Bureau. Cuney was a mulatto whose wealthy, white planter father freed him before the Civil War and arranged for his education in Pennsylvania. He became active in Galveston politics, serving as an alderman and national Republican delegate. In 1889, he was appointed U.S. Collector of Customs in Galveston ‚Äì the highest-ranking appointed position of any African American in the late 19th-century South.

From 1902 through 1965, the Democrat-dominated legislature in Texas had virtually disenfranchised most blacks and many Latinos and poor whites through the imposition of the poll tax and white primaries. Republican support in Texas had been based almost exclusively in free black communities, particularly in Galveston, and in the "German counties" ‚Äì the rural Texas Hill Country inhabited by German immigrants who had opposed slavery.

üîç Click to explore: What was the Shivercrat movement?

+35 points! üîÑ Political realignment begins!

Some analysts suggest that the rebirth of the Republican Party in Texas among white conservatives can be traced to 1952, when Democrat Governor Allan Shivers clashed with the Truman Administration over the federal claim on the Tidelands ‚Äì offshore lands claimed by both Texas and the national government believed to contain oil. Shivers led a movement often known as the Shivercrat movement, which forecast a dramatic change in party alignments a quarter-century later. He worked to help Texas native General Dwight D. Eisenhower carry the state.

üî¥ Republican Dominance Today

As of June 2017, Democrats were the minority party in the state legislature, did not hold any statewide elected offices, and occupied only 11 of the state's 36 congressional seats. When one party controls the governor's office, the state House, and state Senate, that party controls a trifecta. Republicans gained the Texas trifecta in 2003 and have maintained it since, making Texas one of the most Republican states in the U.S. Despite overall Republican dominance, Austin, El Paso, Houston, Dallas, San Antonio, and the Rio Grande Valley remain primarily Democratic.

üîç Click to explore: What does the future hold for Texas politics?

+30 points! üîÆ Changing demographics!

The Hispanic population has continued to increase. As of 2011, the Hispanic population accounted for 38.1% of the state's population (compared to 44.8% for non-Hispanic whites). The state's changing demographics may result in a change in its overall political alignment, as most Hispanic and Latino voters support the Democrat Party. Analysts with Gallup suggest that low turnout among Texas Hispanics is all that enables continued Republican dominance. In 2018, Democrat Beto O'Rourke lost his Senate bid to Ted Cruz by only about 200,000 votes ‚Äì a significant gain for Democrats that challenged notions of Republican dominance.

Party Identification and Political Ideology

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Understand party identification and political ideologies
  • Learn how party identification is measured and why it matters
üí° Political Ideology
A political ideology is a certain set of ethical ideals, principles, doctrines, myths or symbols of a social movement, institution, class or large group that explains how society should work and offers some political and cultural blueprint for a certain social order. Citizens in the U.S. generally classify themselves as adherent to positions along the political spectrum as either liberal, progressive, moderate, or conservative.
üîç Click to explore: What do liberals believe?

+30 points! üîµ Liberal ideology explained!

Liberals advocate strong civil liberties and social progressivism according to which societal practices need to be changed whenever necessary for the greater good of society. They believe that government action is needed in order for people to be as free as possible. The government must thereby ensure the provision of positive rights, protect civil liberties and ensure equality. Liberals commonly reject both laissez-faire capitalism and socialism as means to distribute economic resources. A mixed economy ‚Äì a capitalist free market economy with limited government regulation and intervention ‚Äì is seen as the ideal.

üîç Click to explore: What do conservatives believe?

+30 points! üî¥ Conservative ideology explained!

The word "conservative" comes from "conserve," hence describing those who generally wish to conserve the status quo, conserve morality, or conserve money. A majority of conservatives support tax-cuts and other laissez-faire (reduced governmental interference) policies, oppose same-sex marriage, oppose abortion, oppose stricter gun control laws on the grounds of the Second Amendment, and favor increased military spending. Conservatives tend to favor (racial) color-blindness and oppose affirmative action quotas. They also tend to favor state governments over the federal.

üó≥Ô∏è Party Identification
Party identification refers to the political party with which an individual identifies. It is loyalty to a political party, typically determined by the party that an individual most commonly supports. Some researchers view it as "a form of social identity" or psychological attachment that develops early in life through family and social influences. Others consider it more flexible and based on continued assessment of the political, economic and social environment.
üîç Click to explore: What is partisan polarization?

+30 points! üìä Growing divide revealed!

The past thirty years have brought a dramatic change in the relationship between the two parties as fewer conservative Democrats and liberal Republicans have been elected to office. As political moderates leave the political parties at all levels, the parties have grown farther apart ideologically ‚Äì a result called partisan polarization. This is the degree to which Democrats have become more liberal and Republicans have become more conservative. In politics, partisan polarization may make it increasingly more difficult for politicians to compromise on critical policy issues.

Texas Party Organization

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Understand permanent and temporary party organizations in Texas
  • Learn about official political parties recognized in Texas

Although many Texans claim that they are "registered Republicans" or "registered Democrats," Texas does not have a system of party registration. Registered voters may vote in either the Democrat or Republican primary. One of the most important functions of political parties is to select candidates to run for office under the party label, which is currently accomplished through primary elections.

üìã Official Political Parties in Texas

As of September 2019, the state of Texas officially recognized four political parties:

  • Democratic Party of Texas
  • Republican Party of Texas
  • Libertarian Party of Texas
  • Green Party of Texas

In order to be recognized by the state, a political party must fulfill certain ballot access requirements. Texas does not allow candidates to use political party designations (alternative labels) on the ballot.

üèõÔ∏è Permanent Party Organization
The permanent organization includes precinct chairs elected in the party primary who head precinct conventions and serve on the county executive committee. The county chair leads the county executive committee (composed of the chair and precinct chairs), which runs the county primary and plans county conventions. At the state level, a state executive committee includes a state chair and vice-chair selected every two years at state party conventions. This committee accepts candidate filings, helps raise funds, and establishes party policy.
üîç Click to explore: What is the temporary party organization?

+30 points! üó≥Ô∏è Convention system explained!

The temporary organization includes precinct conventions, where delegates are selected for the county convention and resolutions may be submitted that could become part of the party platform. Delegates chosen at precinct conventions go to county conventions (or district conventions in urban areas), which then elect delegates to the state convention. At state conventions held every other year: nominees are certified for statewide office, a party platform is adopted, and the chair, vice-chair, and state executive committee are elected. In presidential election years, state conventions also select delegates for national conventions and choose presidential electors.

üîç Click to explore: What happens at national conventions?

+30 points! üé™ The media spectacle!

National conventions are typically held between June and September and last four to five days, with days devoted to platform discussion and nights reserved for speeches. Members of the candidate's family speak during the first few days, with the vice-presidential nominee speaking on the next-to-last night and the presidential candidate on the final night. While conventions used to feature high drama, modern conventions have become highly sanitized, orchestrated media events. Speakers often have their speeches prescreened to ensure they don't deviate from the party line. Between 1960 and 2004, viewership declined by half.

Third-Party Movements in Texas

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Discuss third-party movements in Texas history
  • Understand the Tea Party and Occupy movements

At various points in the past 170 years, elites and voters have sought to create alternatives to the existing party system. Political parties that are formed as alternatives to the Republican and Democratic parties are known as third parties, or minor parties. As with many other states, the two-party system in Texas has made it very difficult for third parties to get on the ballot.

‚úä La Raza Unida
La Raza Unida, meaning "united race," was created in the early 1970s to combat growing inequality and dissatisfaction with the Democratic Party typically supported by Mexican-American voters. Jos√© √Ångel Guti√©rrez led the party at its inception in Zavala County. The party won races in Crystal City, Cotulla, and Carrizo Springs by taking advantage of nonpartisan elections. At one point, they won two city council majorities, two school board majorities, and two mayoralties. Although the party did poorly in the 1978 Texas elections, it signified the growing influence of Latinos in the state.
üîç Click to explore: What are the Green and Libertarian parties?

+30 points! üåø Third parties explained!

The Green Party of Texas was founded as the electoral arm for grassroots democracy, social justice, ecological wisdom, peace, and nonviolence. Ralph Nader's 1996 campaign spurred growth. They gained ballot access through 2026 after obtaining 2% of the vote for Railroad Commissioner in 2016.

The Libertarian Party of Texas has emerged as a third-party alternative. Libertarians believe in limited government and are typically considered fiscal conservatives and social liberals. While unsuccessful at the polls, they can influence politics when major parties adopt their positions to win support in run-off elections.

üîç Click to explore: What is the Tea Party movement?

+30 points! üçµ Conservative movement revealed!

Born from the Libertarian Party, the Tea Party movement was launched following a February 19, 2009 call by CNBC reporter Rick Santelli on the floor of the Chicago Mercantile Exchange. The movement allied with the Republican Party and has had great influence in Texas due to its antitax messaging. The Tea Party is hostile to government intervention, especially taxation and business regulation, viewing them as threats to capitalism and democracy. Some factions are active on social issues, opposing same-sex marriage and abortion rights. In 2012, 86% of Tea Party members voted Republican. Tea Party activist Grover Norquist exacted promises from many Republicans to oppose any bill that sought to raise taxes.

‚úä The Occupy Movement
Created following government bailouts in 2008, the Occupy movement has held demonstrations in Austin and other major Texas cities, protesting the influence of big corporations and Wall Street on American politics. The movement aimed at limiting the influence of Wall Street and big corporations in American politics.

Candidate and Delegate Selection

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Compare and contrast primary and caucus candidate selection processes
  • Understand the Texas Two-Step and current delegate allocation

Presidential candidates in the United States are not directly nominated via primary elections; instead, presidential nominees are formally nominated at political party conventions. Presidential preference primary elections and caucuses are held in each state to determine how that state's delegation will vote during the nominating convention.

üó≥Ô∏è Texas Primary System
In 2016, Texas' political parties conducted open presidential preference primaries ‚Äì voters were not required to be a member of a party to participate in its primary. Texas law requires parties to conduct open primary elections for state and county offices, as well as congressional offices. During the nineteenth century, candidates were nominated at party conventions, but early in the 20th century, the state moved to the primary. Winners in primary contests are determined by majority vote, with a runoff between the top two if no candidate receives a majority.
üîç Click to explore: What was the "Texas Two-Step"?

+30 points! üíÉ A unique Texas system!

The Texas Two-Step (named after the partner dance) was a process where Texans were required to first vote in the primary election in order to be eligible for participation in party caucuses where delegates were selected. The Texas Democratic Party abandoned this former caucus-based system in 2015 after the 2008 Democratic County Conventions produced great confusion. Both Clinton and Obama supporters had concerns about how these conventions were conducted, with the most common complaint being that delegates were not being apportioned based on Precinct Convention results.

üîç Click to explore: How do the parties currently allocate delegates?

+30 points! üìä Delegate math explained!

Republicans: Have a winner-take-all provision, but the chances any candidate will get all delegates are very small. A candidate would need more than 50% statewide AND in each of the 36 congressional districts. Absent that, a pro-rata system allocates delegates according to votes received.

Democrats: No longer use caucuses. After primary votes are counted, delegates are awarded among candidates who received 15% or more, in proportion to votes received. A Democrat could only get all Texas delegates by winning 85% statewide AND 85% in each of Texas' 31 state Senate districts ‚Äì making it even harder than for Republicans.
