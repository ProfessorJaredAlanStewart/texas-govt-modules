What is Public Policy?

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Discuss important public policy issues in Texas
  • Understand the underlying controversies in making public policy

Public policy is the broad strategy government uses to do its job, the relatively stable set of purposive governmental behaviors that address matters of concern to some part of society. Most policy outcomes are the result of considerable debate, compromise, and refinement that happen over years and are finalized only after input from multiple institutions within government.

üìã Public Policy
Public policy is the relatively stable set of purposive governmental actions that address matters of concern to some part of society. It is a guide to legislative action that is more or less fixed for long periods of time, not just short-term fixes or single legislative acts. Simply put, public policy is anything the government does to achieve a particular outcome.
üîç Click to explore: How is public policy formed?

+30 points! üèõÔ∏è The policy development process!

Policy doesn't happen by accident, and it is rarely formed simply as the result of campaign promises of a single elected official. Most policy outcomes result from considerable debate, compromise, and refinement over years with input from multiple institutions. For example, health care reform (the ACA) was developed after years of analysis, reflection on existing policy, and trial implementation at the state level. What may seem sudden was actually the product of decades of work by both parties, including proposals from Mitt Romney when he was governor of Massachusetts.

Public policy decisions impact our lives in many ways. As a result, we should understand how policies are formed, budgeted, implemented, and evaluated. We should also know who the policymakers are and be able to measure the effectiveness of policies that have been made.

üîç Click to explore: What are the key characteristics of public policy?

+30 points! ‚úÖ Defining features revealed!

Public policy has several important characteristics:

  • Stability: It is relatively stable and fixed for long periods
  • Broad concern: It deals with issues affecting large segments of society, not just individuals
  • Beyond action: It includes government behaviors AND the outcomes those actions create
  • Inaction counts: Policy can be made when government refuses to act, maintaining the status quo

For example, much of the debate over gun control centers on Congress's unwillingness to act, even when public opinion supports changes. The 2004 expiration of the Federal Assault Weapons Ban resulted from lawmakers' inaction.

Types of Goods and Policy Categories

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Understand different types of goods: private, public, common, and toll
  • Identify distributive, regulatory, and redistributive policies

Economists use the term "goods" to describe the range of commodities, services, and systems that help us satisfy our wants or needs. Understanding different types of goods helps explain why government involvement is necessary in some areas.

üè† Private Goods
Private goods can be owned by a particular person or group and are excluded from use by others, typically by means of a price. They are finite and can run out if overused. Your home or apartment is a private good because you pay rent or mortgage payments for the right to live there. The excludable and finite nature of private goods makes them tradable in the marketplace.
üîç Click to explore: What are public goods and why do they need government?

+30 points! üåç Public goods explained!

Public goods (sometimes called collective goods) are not excludable and are essentially infinite. Examples include air, national security, and geographic regions like forests. The problem is that since no one owns them, no one has a financial interest in protecting their long-term value. Without government regulation, a factory owner can freely pollute the air or water since they have no responsibility for pollution once it moves elsewhere. This is why government involvement is necessary to protect these shared resources.

üé£ Common Goods & The Tragedy of the Commons
Common goods (like fisheries, forests, and water) are not excludable but may be finite. Without regulation, someone can hunt all migratory birds or deplete a fishery, eliminating future breeding stocks. The situation in which individuals exhaust a common resource by acting in their own immediate self-interest is called the tragedy of the commons.
üîç Click to explore: What are the three types of public policy?

+30 points! üìä Policy categories revealed!

Theodore Lowi categorized policy based on costs and benefits:

  • Distributive Policy: Collects from many, benefits few (e.g., highways, railroads, agricultural subsidies, Pell grants)
  • Regulatory Policy: Concentrated costs, diffuse benefits (e.g., environmental protection, public health and safety)
  • Redistributive Policy: Transfers resources from one group to another, like "Robin Hood" (e.g., Medicaid, TANF, SNAP, Head Start)
üöÇ Texas & The Transcontinental Railroad

The Transcontinental Railroad illustrates distributive policy. In the 1860s, the U.S. government chartered the Central Pacific and Union Pacific Railroads, providing resources and land grants to facilitate construction. Publicly owned land was distributed to private citizens who could use it for their own gain, while simultaneously providing a national transportation network. Texas continues to have more railroad mileage than any other state and the largest number of railroad employees.

The Policy-Making Process

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Describe the five stages in making public policy in Texas
  • Understand the cyclical nature of policymaking

Policymaking is a cyclical process. It begins with recognition of a significant public problem and an organized call to government action. The process is a cycle because the evaluation stage should feed back into the earlier stages, informing future decisions about the policy.

1Ô∏è‚É£ Problem Definition
The first stage focuses public attention on a public problem or issue. Problem identification specifies the issues that merit discussion and necessitates considering alternative solutions. For example, officials may agree that childhood obesity is a problem, but solutions might range from school physical education programs to removing soda machines from schools. Our ideology‚Äîliberal, conservative, libertarian‚Äîplays a critical role in defining what is "good" policy.
üîç Click to explore: What is agenda setting?

+30 points! üìã Stage 2 explained!

Agenda Setting is the second stage. Not all issues make it onto the governmental agenda because there is only so much attention government can pay. One important task for a policy advocate is to frame their issue in a compelling way. For example, health care reform was made salient by framing it in terms of health care access and the percentage of people without insurance. In recent years, the Texas official policy agenda has included educational finance, property tax reform, medical malpractice reform, welfare reform, and corrections policy reform.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Policy Adoption
Once a proposed policy has been added to the agenda, the elected branches of government consider one specific solution and decide whether to pass it. Policy can be made through legislation signed by the governor or when a state agency changes a rule or regulation. This stage is the most visible and usually garners the most press coverage.
üîç Click to explore: Implementation and Evaluation

+30 points! üîÑ Stages 4 & 5 revealed!

Implementation (Stage 4): Government agencies begin making the policy work. They establish procedures, write guidance documents, and issue grants-in-aid. Identifying the appropriate agency is crucial. Budgetary policy plays a major role and could determine a policy's success or failure.

Evaluation (Stage 5): All policies must be evaluated for effectiveness. Evaluation asks, "How well did this policy do what we designed it to do?" A 2011 study found that abstinence-only education actually increased teen pregnancy rates‚Äîthe opposite of its intended goal! Evaluation may reveal needs for revisions, implementation changes, or new policies entirely.

Civil Liberties and Civil Rights Policy

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Understand affirmative action policies in Texas
  • Examine gun rights and gun control debates
  • Explore civil rights issues in Texas
üìö Affirmative Action
Affirmative action refers to policies adopted by governments and institutions to take proactive measures to increase the proportion of historically disadvantaged minority groups. These have taken forms including quotas (banned by the Supreme Court in 1978's Regents v. Bakke), extra outreach efforts, and student financial aid for minorities. As of March 2015, 109 of 577 public four-year universities considered race in admissions, though this practice has been banned in eight states.
üîç Click to explore: What are gun rights in Texas?

+30 points! üî´ Second Amendment in Texas!

The Second Amendment reads: "A well regulated Militia being necessary to the security of a free State, the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed." In Texas, this right belongs to "every citizen" by the Texas Constitution. Estimates of Texas gun ownership range from 35% to 43% of the population‚Äîabove the national average.

Eight new gun laws went into effect September 1, 2019, including allowing guns in places of worship (unless banned with proper signage), during disasters, and in rented property. Campus carry laws became effective August 1, 2016 for 4-year universities and August 1, 2017 for community colleges, pertaining to concealed carry only.

üîç Click to explore: What happened with the "bathroom bill"?

+30 points! üöª Civil rights debate!

Several bills were filed in the 2017 Texas Legislature regarding transgender bathroom access. Senator Lois Kolkhorst sponsored SB6, championed by Lt. Governor Dan Patrick. The Texas Senate passed SB6 (regular session) and SB3 (Special Session) by 21-10 largely along party lines. SB6 would have limited bathroom access based on the sex listed on one's birth certificate. However, in August 2017, the Texas Legislature adjourned without the House voting on any "transgender bathroom bill." This illustrates how policy can be made (or not made) through legislative inaction.

Education Policy in Texas

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Understand K-12 public education funding in Texas
  • Explore higher education and school choice options

One of the most important functions of state government is providing and funding public education. Texas has a very large and complex public school system, with an equally complex school finance system. Three issues have played a major role in shaping education: desegregation, funding equity, and finding the formula for educational excellence.

üìö K-12 Public Education
The Texas public school system (prekindergarten through grade 12) operates within districts governed by locally elected school boards and superintendents. In 2018, $52.3 billion in state and local money went to 5.4 million students in 1,019 traditional school districts and 171 charter districts.
üîç Click to explore: How is school funding calculated?

+30 points! üí∞ The basic allotment!

The state of Texas guarantees every school district a certain amount of funding for each student, called the basic allotment. State lawmakers determine the base number per student, which was $5,140 in 2018. Many educators argue that the state should regularly increase that base number to match inflation to provide schools with needed funding; however, the basic allotment per student has not changed substantially since 2010.

üè´ Texas Higher Education

Texas' higher education system is composed of 268 colleges and universities: 107 public institutions, 73 nonprofit private schools, and 88 for-profit private institutions. At public four-year colleges, 27.6% graduated within four years, while 51.7% graduated within six years. Hispanic students comprised approximately 34.0% of the total postsecondary student population. Texas was one of five states where teaching/research assistants alone outnumbered full-time faculty.

üîç Click to explore: What is school choice?

+30 points! üè´ Educational alternatives!

School choice programs provide alternatives to parents who don't want to send children to assigned public schools. Options include:

  • Open enrollment policies
  • Magnet schools
  • Charter schools (723 in Texas in 2015-16, enrolling about 282,900 students)
  • Vouchers, tax credits, and Education Savings Accounts (ESAs)

As of 2016, Texas provided no financial assistance for parents sending children to private schools. Charter schools in Texas cannot charge tuition or impose special entrance requirements; students are admitted through a lottery if demand exceeds spaces.

Energy and Environmental Policy

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Understand energy policy and fracking in Texas
  • Explore environmental protection regulations

Energy policy involves governmental actions affecting the production, distribution, and consumption of energy. These policies include legislation, regulation, taxes, incentives for energy production or use, and standards for energy efficiency. A variety of factors affect feasibility, including available natural resources, geography, and consumer needs.

üõ¢Ô∏è Hydraulic Fracturing (Fracking)
Hydraulic fracturing, also known as fracking, is a method of oil and natural gas extraction that involves injecting fluid into subterranean rock formations at high pressure. The Oil and Gas Division within the Texas Railroad Commission is responsible for regulating fracking in Texas. The fluid (frac fluid) contains 98-99.5% water and sand; only 0.5-2% is chemical additives used to stop microorganism growth, prevent corrosion, and reduce pressure.
üîç Click to explore: What are Texas' energy resources?

+30 points! ‚õΩ Texas oil and gas!

Texas overlies portions of major oil and gas formations:

  • Anadarko Basin
  • Palo Duro Basin
  • Permian Basin
  • Barnett Shale
  • Eagle Ford Shale
  • Haynesville-Bossier Shale

As of February 2017, Texas had 279,615 active oil and gas wells. According to the Texas Railroad Commission, fracking began in Texas in the 1950s. Texas regulations require operators to disclose chemicals used in fracking on FracFocus.org, though trade secrets may be withheld.

üîç Click to explore: What federal laws regulate fracking?

+30 points! üèõÔ∏è Federal environmental oversight!

While states have primary regulatory authority, oil and gas operators must meet federal requirements:

  • Clean Air Act: Regulates air pollutants during production
  • Clean Water Act: Regulates pollution discharges into surface waters; requires permits for produced water discharge
  • CERCLA (Superfund): Requires reporting hazardous substance releases; allows EPA to investigate and require restoration of affected areas

Environmental policy aims to conserve natural resources by balancing environmental protection with economic growth, property rights, public health, and energy production.

Healthcare and Social Welfare Policy

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Understand Medicare, Medicaid, and the ACA in Texas
  • Explore the social safety net and welfare programs

Healthcare policy affects not only the cost citizens pay for care, but also their access to care and the quality of care received. A top concern for policymakers is the rising cost of healthcare, which has placed an increasing strain on consumers' disposable income and state budgets.

üè• Medicare
Medicare is an entitlement program funded through payroll taxes. Its purpose is to ensure senior citizens and retirees have access to low-cost health care. It provides three major forms of coverage: guaranteed insurance for hospitalization, fee-based supplemental coverage for doctor visits, and a prescription drug benefit. Medicare faces the problem that health care costs are rising significantly faster than inflation.
üîç Click to explore: How does Medicaid work in Texas?

+30 points! üíä Medicaid explained!

Medicaid is a formula-based health insurance program‚Äîbeneficiaries must demonstrate they fall within a particular income category. It's jointly funded by federal and state governments, with eligibility, benefits, and administration managed by states within federal guidelines. Texas' Medicaid program provides medical insurance to low-income people and individuals with disabilities.

Related programs include CHIP (Children's Health Insurance Program) covering low-income children above the poverty line, and TexCare offering additional children's health insurance through Medicaid and SKIP (State Kids Insurance Program).

üõ°Ô∏è The Social Safety Net

During the Great Depression, the U.S. created programs constituting a "social safety net" for millions who lost jobs, homes, and savings. The Texas Department of Public Welfare was established in 1939 during the New Deal. As of December 2016, roughly 14% of Texas residents (approximately 4 million people) received Social Security benefits. About 87% of Texans aged 65+ received benefits. Benefits totaled roughly $4.85 billion for December 2016, averaging $1,206 per recipient per month.

üîç Click to explore: What is the Affordable Care Act?

+30 points! üìã The ACA in Texas!

The Affordable Care Act (ACA), also known as Obamacare, was signed into law in 2010 by President Barack Obama. It facilitates purchase of health insurance through exchanges, tax credits, and subsidies. A 2012 Supreme Court ruling made Medicaid expansion voluntary for states. The law requires insurers to cover standard benefits and prohibits coverage denials based on preexisting conditions.

Texas led other states in efforts to combat what some called federal government takeover of healthcare. Even before the ACA, more than 50% of all healthcare expenditures in the U.S. already came from federal programs like Medicare and Medicaid.

Influencing Policy Decisions

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Understand the role of policy advocates and policy analysts
  • Learn how citizens can influence policy decisions

Two important but overlapping groups defend their preferred policy changes: policy advocates and policy analysts. Understanding how these groups operate helps citizens engage more effectively in the policy process.

üì¢ Policy Advocates
Policy advocates actively work to propose or maintain public policy. They hold a normative position‚Äîa conviction about what should or ought to be done. They begin with an objective and try to shape proposals that accomplish that goal. Facts, evidence, and analysis are tools for convincing policymakers of their proposals' benefits. The most effective advocates are usually hired professionals who form lobbying groups or think tanks.
üîç Click to explore: How does AARP advocate for seniors?

+30 points! üë¥ Lobbying in action!

AARP (formerly American Association of Retired Persons) is a lobbying group that frequently takes on advocacy roles. Its primary job is to convince government to provide more resources and services to senior citizens, including lower health care costs and safety of Social Security payments.

In 2002, AARP surprised many by supporting the Republican prescription drug plan over the Democratic proposal. While Democrats argued their position was better for individuals, AARP reasoned the Republican plan had a better chance of passing. AARP created a new benefit it hoped could later be expanded, rather than fighting for an extreme position that might leave seniors with nothing.

üìä Policy Analysts
Policy analysts try to identify all possible choices available to a decision maker and gauge their impacts if implemented. Rather than encouraging implementation of specific options, they ensure decision makers are fully informed about implications of their decisions. Because advocates have incentives to understate costs and overstate benefits, policy analysis tends to be highly politicized.
üîç Click to explore: How can YOU influence policy?

+30 points! ‚úä Get connected!

What is your passion? Is there an aspect of society you think should be changed? Become a public policy advocate for it! Ways to participate:

  • Contact your elected representatives
  • Join advocacy organizations related to your interests
  • Participate in public hearings and town halls
  • Vote in every election‚Äîlocal, state, and federal
  • Use government petition platforms like "We the People"

Think tanks like the Texas Public Policy Foundation, Cato Institute, and Brookings Institution provide policy analysis from various perspectives.

üìö Chapter Glossary

Distributive policy: A policy that collects payments broadly but concentrates direct benefits on relatively few.
Entitlement: A program that guarantees benefits to members of a specific group or segment of the population.
Libertarians: People who believe government almost always operates less efficiently than the private sector and that its actions should be kept to a minimum.
Medicaid: A federal and state program financing medical services for low-income citizens.
Medicare: An entitlement health insurance program for older people and retirees.
Policy advocates: People who actively work to propose or maintain public policy.
Policy analysts: People who identify all possible choices available to a decision-maker and assess potential impact of each.
Public policy: The broad strategy government uses to do its job; the relatively stable set of purposive governmental behaviors that address matters of concern to society.
Redistributive policy: A policy in which costs are borne by a relatively small number of groups, but benefits are enjoyed by a different group.
Regulatory policy: A policy that regulates companies and organizations in a way that protects the public.
Safety net: A way to provide for members of society experiencing economic hardship.
Social Security: A social welfare policy for people who no longer receive income from employment.
